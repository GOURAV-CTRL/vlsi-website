Complements & Signed Numbers

Digital Design — Module 01, Video 02

Digital circuits need a way to represent negative numbers using nothing but 0s and 1s. This is where complements come in — they let hardware perform subtraction using the very same adder circuit built for addition, which massively simplifies ALU design.

1R's and (R−1)'s Complements

For any number system of radix R, there are two standard complements:

  • (R−1)'s complement — subtract each digit from (R−1).
  • R's complement — take the (R−1)'s complement and add 1 to the least significant digit.

These aren't just a binary trick — the same idea applies across systems:

System(R−1)'s ComplementR's Complement
Binary1's complement2's complement
Octal7's complement8's complement
Decimal9's complement10's complement
Why bother?
Once a negative number is stored as a complement, A − B can be computed as A + (complement of B) — the circuit only ever needs to add.

2Ways to Represent Signed Numbers

① Sign Magnitude

The MSB is reserved purely as a sign flag — 0 for positive, 1 for negative — and the remaining bits store the magnitude directly.

Drawback — Zero has two valid encodings, +0 and −0, which wastes a code and complicates comparison logic in hardware.

② 1's Complement

Positive numbers are stored as-is. Negative numbers are formed by flipping every bit of the corresponding positive value.

Drawback — Just like sign magnitude, this scheme still produces two representations of zero (0000 and 1111 for a 4-bit example), inherited from the same fundamental issue.

③ 2's Complement

Negative numbers are formed by taking the 1's complement and adding 1. This is the method actually used across essentially all modern digital hardware.

Why it wins
There is exactly one representation for zero — the ambiguity of +0 / −0 disappears entirely.
Addition and subtraction both reduce to plain binary addition, with no special-case circuitry needed.
Key takeaway — 2's complement is the industry-standard method for representing signed numbers because it removes the dual-zero problem of sign magnitude and 1's complement, while keeping arithmetic circuits as simple as a single adder.
