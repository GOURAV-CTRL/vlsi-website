Introduction to Boolean Algebra

Digital Design — Module 02, Video 05

Boolean algebra is the mathematical framework that lets us describe, simplify, and prove the correctness of digital logic. Every gate-level circuit is really just a Boolean expression turned into hardware, so mastering these laws is the foundation for everything that follows.

1Boolean Values & Operators

Unlike ordinary algebra, Boolean algebra works with just two values — 0 (false) and 1 (true) — combined using three basic operators:

  • AND ( · ) — output is 1 only if all inputs are 1
  • OR ( + ) — output is 1 if at least one input is 1
  • NOT ( ' or ‾ ) — inverts the input

2Basic Postulates

Identity & Null Laws
A + 0 = A   |   A · 1 = A
A + 1 = 1   |   A · 0 = 0
Idempotent & Complement Laws
A + A = A   |   A · A = A
A + A' = 1   |   A · A' = 0

3Major Algebraic Laws

4De Morgan's Theorems

These two theorems are the most heavily used identities in digital design — they let us convert between AND-based and OR-based expressions, which is essential when building circuits out of universal gates.

Theorem 1
(A · B)' = A' + B'
"NOT of AND" = "OR of NOTs"
Theorem 2
(A + B)' = A' · B'
"NOT of OR" = "AND of NOTs"
Why this matters — Boolean algebra isn't just theory — it's the tool used to minimize logic expressions before building them in hardware. Fewer terms and literals directly translate to fewer gates, less area, and lower power on a real chip.
Key takeaway — Every simplification technique used later — SOP/POS reduction, K-maps, and even RTL-level optimization — traces back to these same Boolean postulates and De Morgan's theorems.
